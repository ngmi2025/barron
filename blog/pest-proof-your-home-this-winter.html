5 Ways to Pest-Proof Your Home This Winter

As autumn arrives and temperatures drop, rats, mice, cockroaches and other pests start looking for warmth and shelter indoors. Prevention is always better — and cheaper — than cure. Here are five practical steps to protect your home.

LM

the Barron team

Why Winter Pest-Proofing Matters

Every autumn, as the weather turns cold and damp, pests that have been happily living outdoors start seeking warmth, shelter and food inside our homes. Rats, mice, cockroaches, cluster flies and even squirrels will exploit any weakness in your property’s defences to get in.

The good news? Most pest problems are preventable. A few hours spent proofing your home now can save you hundreds of pounds — and a lot of stress — later on. Whether you live in Suffolk County, Nassau County, The Hamptons or anywhere across Long Island, these five steps will help keep unwanted visitors out this winter.

1. Seal Gaps and Entry Points

This is the single most important thing you can do. Mice can squeeze through a gap the width of a pencil — that’s just 6mm. Rats need only 15mm, roughly the diameter of a five-pence coin. If you can slide a biro into a gap, a mouse can get through it.

Check carefully around:

  • Pipes, cables and vents where they enter the building
  • Gaps around doors and windows, especially at the base
  • Air bricks — fit mesh covers to keep rodents out whilst maintaining ventilation
  • Where soil pipes meet the wall
  • Gaps under doors — fit brush strips or door sweeps

For smaller gaps, pack wire wool tightly into the hole and seal over it with expanding foam. Rodents can’t chew through the wire wool. For larger gaps, use cement or metal plate. Never rely on expanding foam alone — rats and mice will chew straight through it.

2. Secure Your Food Sources

Pests come indoors for three things: warmth, shelter and food. Remove the food and you remove a major incentive. Here’s what to do:

  • Store dried goods in sealed containers. Use glass or thick plastic containers, not thin bags or cardboard boxes that rodents can chew through.
  • Don’t leave pet food out overnight. Pick up bowls and store pet food in sealed bins.
  • Clean up crumbs and spills promptly. Pay particular attention to behind cookers and under kitchen units.
  • Keep bird feeders away from the house and use squirrel-proof models. Spilt bird seed is one of the biggest attractants for rats.
  • Maintain your compost bin. Keep it well-managed and never leave it open. Turn it regularly and avoid adding cooked food.

3. Manage Your Garden and Outdoor Spaces

Your garden can either be a pest highway or a first line of defence. A bit of autumn tidying makes a big difference:

  • Cut back vegetation touching the house. Overhanging branches, climbing plants and dense shrubs against walls give rats and mice a direct route to your roof and loft.
  • Clear leaf litter from around the foundations. Piles of leaves provide cover and nesting material.
  • Store firewood away from the building and raised off the ground on a rack. Woodpiles against the house are ideal harbourage for rodents and insects.
  • Fix leaking taps and clear standing water. Pests need water too — removing water sources makes your property less attractive.
  • Keep bins sealed with tight-fitting lids. Overflowing or open wheelie bins are an easy food source for rats and foxes.

4. Check Your Loft and Roof

Lofts are one of the most common places we find rodent activity, and they’re often the first point of entry. Rats and squirrels are excellent climbers and will exploit any weakness in your roofline.

Take a torch up into your loft and check for:

  • Gaps where roof tiles meet the eaves — even small gaps can let mice in
  • Damaged or rotting soffit boards and fascias
  • Gaps around plumbing vents and soil pipes that pass through the roof
  • Signs of activity — droppings, gnaw marks or shredded nesting material
  • Disturbed loft insulation — rodents will tunnel through it and use it for nesting

5. Don’t Forget Your Drains

This is the one most people overlook, and it’s one of the most common rat entry points we deal with. Broken, cracked or collapsed drains give rats a direct route from the sewer system into your property.

Rats are strong swimmers and can navigate through water traps with ease. If drain seals are faulty or missing, rats can even enter your home via the toilet. It sounds unlikely, but we see it regularly.

If you keep finding rats in your property despite treatment, there’s a good chance the problem is coming from the drains. A CCTV drain survey can identify breaks, displacements and missing interceptor traps that are letting rats in. Barron Pest Control can arrange this for you as part of a comprehensive treatment plan.

When to Call a Professional

Prevention is always the first step, but if you’ve already spotted signs of pest activity — droppings, gnaw marks, scratching noises at night, or a musty smell — then proofing alone won’t solve the problem. You need to deal with the existing infestation first.

A qualified pest controller can identify exactly what you’re dealing with, treat it effectively using professional-grade methods, and then proof your property to stop it happening again. Shop-bought traps and poisons often make the problem worse by scattering pests to new areas of the building.
